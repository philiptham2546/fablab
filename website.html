Welcome to the FABLab Young Adult Capital Punishment visualizer!

In a series of seminal judgments, the US Supreme Court deemed the imposition of capital punishment and mandatory life without parole (LWOP) for juveniles unconstitutional (Roper v. Simmons, 2005; Graham v. Florida, 2010; Miller v. Alabama, 2012) due to, among other factors, their level of cognitive and psychosocial (im)maturity. Recent evidence suggests that, at least in some circumstances, Young Adults, those from 18 to 21 years of age, show similar neurocognitive capacity to minors, suggesting that the legal protections offered to juveniles should also be extended to this age category.

In Roper (2005), the Supreme Court identified "evolving standards of decency" as a key argument for banning the juvenile death penalty. In particular, it considered the number of States where juvenile death penalty was either formally prohibited or practiced infrequently as key to determining such standards. In a similar vein, this visualization keeps track of the number of Young Adults sentenced to the death penalty across the States. It is based on information from 2018 and updated through end-2023.

A guide to using the tool: White states are those where the death penalty is still legal. Gray states are those where the death penalty is illegal. States with diagonals are those where a gubernatorial moratorium has been placed. Pink states have sentenced at least one Young Adult to death since 2018. Click on each state to access data that will be displayed in the panel on the right.
